# Past Performance Guide

## What the Numbers Mean
When reading a past performance line you will see data in this order:
- Date of race
- Track name
- Distance
- Surface (D=dirt, T=turf, AW=all weather)
- Finish position out of how many horses
- Lengths behind the winner
- Speed figure (Beyer or Equibase figure — higher is faster)
- Jockey
- Weight carried
- Odds at post time

## What to Look For
### Improving horses
- Speed figures going up race over race = improving form
- A horse that ran 85, 88, 91 in last three races is peaking at the right time
- Most dangerous horse in any field is one whose figures are climbing

### Declining horses
- Speed figures going down = avoid
- A horse that ran 95, 90, 84 is regressing. Odds may not reflect this yet.

### The Last Race Trap
- Never judge a horse solely on its last race
- Bad trip last time = forgive it. Look at the race notes.
- Big win last time at a weaker track = downgrade it

### Class
- A horse dropping from Grade 1 to Grade 2 = class dropper, dangerous
- A horse moving up from a maiden win to stakes = class question mark
- Always note what level the horse last competed at

### Layoffs
- Less than 30 days between races = fresh
- 30 to 60 days = normal
- 60 to 180 days = watch the first race back carefully
- Over 180 days = significant concern unless trainer has good stats with layoff horses

### Track Variants
- A fast track produces faster times than an off track
- Always compare speed figures at the same track when possible
- A 95 on a fast track at Churchill Downs is worth more than a 95 on a sloppy track at a minor track

## Key Speed Figure Rules
- Never use a single figure. Use the average of last three races.
- A horse with consistent figures beats an inconsistent horse with one big number
- Figures above 100 at Churchill Downs are elite
- Derby winners typically need a figure of 105 or higher somewhere in their past performances
