# Situations Where Heshie Should Be Very Cautious

## The High Caution List
These are situations where the default answer is to pass the race or fade the horse — but context matters. In horse racing almost anything can happen. Strong caution is appropriate; absolute rules are not.

### Strongly Avoid Betting These Horses to Win

**Heavy favorites below 2-1 in large fields**
The math rarely works. A 1-1 favorite needs to win more than 50 percent of the time to show profit. Favorites win about 33 percent of the time. The odds rarely justify betting heavy favorites to win.

**First time starters in route races**
A horse that has not raced before running a mile or more is a complete unknown. The public often bets these horses based on morning workout times. Workouts are not races. This is a high risk situation — pass unless you have a strong angle.

**Horses with three or more consecutive declining speed figures**
A horse running 98, 93, 87 in its last three races is in serious trouble. Be very cautious betting a horse in declining form regardless of how good its connections are.

**Horses shipping from turf to dirt for the first time with no dirt pedigree**
Surface changes are risky. If a horse has only raced on turf and has no dirt sire or dam sire, the move to dirt is a major question mark. Use only in exceptional circumstances.

**Horses coming back in less than 7 days**
A horse racing back within a week was likely entered as a secondary target. Trainers rarely peak horses for back to back races. The horse running 6 days ago is unlikely to be the main event today.

**Horses with post positions 17 or higher in full fields of 18 or more**
The historical record is clear. Outside posts in large fields at one mile or more rarely win. The traffic and extra distance traveled make it very difficult — though not impossible with the right pace setup.

### Situations to Strongly Avoid

**When you do not understand why the horse is bet down**
If a horse drops from 15-1 to 4-1 and you cannot find a reason, be very cautious about chasing the money. Sharp money moves odds but you need to understand the angle before following it.

**When the track condition is extremely off and you have no mud data**
A sloppy or muddy track completely changes the race. If Heshie does not have mud pedigree data on the horses in the field, the odds rarely justify a bet — strongly consider passing rather than guessing.

**When two horses are so closely matched you cannot separate them**
If your analysis says horse A and horse B are essentially equal, that is rarely a betting race. Save the money for a race where you have a clear edge.

**The last race of the day when your bankroll is up**
Protect your profits. There is always a temptation to bet the last race with winnings. The last race of the day is not special. Apply the same standards you apply to every other race.

**Any race where Heshie says the word guess**
If Heshie uses the word guess in his analysis it means the data is insufficient. Insufficient data is a high risk situation — only proceed in exceptional circumstances.
