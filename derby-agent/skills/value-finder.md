# Skill: Finding Value and Overlays

## What is an Overlay?
An overlay is a horse whose true probability of winning is higher than its odds suggest.
If a horse has a 20 percent chance of winning it should be 4-1.
If that horse is 8-1 it is a significant overlay. Bet it.
If that horse is 2-1 it is an underlay. Avoid it.

## How to Calculate True Probability
Divide 1 by (odds plus 1) to get implied probability.
- 4-1 = 1 divided by 5 = 20 percent chance
- 9-1 = 1 divided by 10 = 10 percent chance
- 2-1 = 1 divided by 3 = 33 percent chance

## Common Overlay Situations

### The Unpopular Trainer
Public bettors follow famous trainer names. When a lesser known trainer has a sharp horse, the odds are often inflated. Always research trainer win percentage at the specific track before dismissing an unknown name.

### The Bad Post that Was Not That Bad
Public overpenalizes outside posts in some races. If a closer draws post 14 in a race, the public may dismiss it. But closers do not need good posts the same way speed horses do.

### The Horse Coming Off a Bad Trip
A horse that finished 6th last time but had traffic trouble, was blocked, or stumbled at the start is not a bad horse. It is a horse that had bad luck. The public sees the finish position. You see the trip.

### The Layoff Horse with a Sharp Trainer
Public undervalues horses returning from a layoff unless the trainer is known for winning first time back. Research the trainer's stats with layoff horses specifically.

### The Class Dropper
A horse dropping from a Grade 1 to an allowance race is often the best horse in the field. Public sometimes ignores class droppers assuming they are unsound. Often they are just being placed to win.

## Common Underlay Situations to Avoid

### The Heavily Bet Favorite
Any horse below 2-1 in a field of 8 or more is almost never a value bet. The payout does not justify the risk.

### The Famous Name Bias
Horses trained by Pletcher, Baffert, or Cox get bet down by name alone. Their horses are good but not always as short as the public makes them.

### The Last Race Winner
A horse that won last time gets bet heavily regardless of whether the win was impressive or the competition was weak. Always check the quality of the last race before following the money.

## The Value Checklist
Before every bet ask:
1. What are the odds?
2. What do I think the true probability is?
3. Is this an overlay or an underlay?
4. If it is not an overlay, pass.
